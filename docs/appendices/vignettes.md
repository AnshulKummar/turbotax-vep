# Customer Vignettes

> Each vignette below is a fictional composite based on recurring friction patterns observed across public review platforms. Names and details are synthetic. These vignettes are designed to make the friction themes from PRD Section 4 concrete and to illustrate how the proposed Virtual Expert Platform would change the outcome.

---

## Vignette 1: The RSU Double-Count (Theme 1 --- Expert Quality and Accuracy Errors)

**Priya Sharma**, 34, is a senior software engineer at a publicly traded company. She vests $95,000 in RSUs during TY2025. Her W-2 Box 1 already includes the RSU income as ordinary compensation. Her 1099-B reports the same shares sold at vesting. Her TurboTax Live Full Service expert enters both amounts as income, inflating her AGI by $95,000. The resulting return shows $6,200 less in refund than she expected. Priya does not catch the error; she trusts the expert. Five months later, the IRS sends a CP2000 notice --- but in this case, the IRS's automated matching actually agrees with the correct lower income, creating a confusing cross-signal. Priya spends 14 hours and $1,800 in CPA fees unwinding the error. The total cost: $1,800 in remediation, the lost time value, and permanent erosion of trust in the Live product.

**What the proposed platform would have done differently:** The deterministic rules engine (B3) includes an RSU double-count detection rule that cross-references W-2 Box 1 compensation codes against 1099-B proceeds for same-day-sale RSU lots. The risk register would have flagged this as a high-confidence anomaly before the expert ever opened the return. The expert workbench would have surfaced it as the number-one item on the ranked risk register with a dollar-impact estimate and a direct citation to the relevant IRS guidance.

## Vignette 2: The Auto-Closed Case (Theme 2 --- Communication Breakdowns and Case Auto-Closure)

**Marcus Williams**, 41, is a freelance graphic designer filing Full Service for the first time. He uploads his 1099-NEC forms, estimated tax payment receipts, and home office documentation on March 28. His case is assigned on April 1. On April 4, the system generates a document request for his prior year return. Marcus does not see the notification because it lands in his email spam folder. On April 18 --- seven days before the filing deadline --- his case is auto-closed for non-responsiveness. Marcus discovers this on April 20 when he logs in to check status. He calls support, waits 2.5 hours, and is told he must start a new case. The new expert is unavailable until April 24. Marcus files for an extension, losing access to his expected $3,400 refund for five additional months and paying $650 for a rush CPA engagement to validate the extension filing.

**What the proposed platform would have done differently:** The escalation panel (I1) would have shown the expert the auto-close countdown and the customer's responsiveness status. The platform would have triggered a multi-channel notification (email, SMS, in-app) before any auto-close action. The expert workbench would have surfaced the case as "at risk" at least 72 hours before the auto-close threshold, giving the expert time to call the customer directly.

## Vignette 3: The Generalist on a K-1 (Theme 3 --- Generalist Routed to Complex Return)

**David and Lisa Chen**, both 38, have a moderately complex return: W-2 income, a K-1 from a real estate syndication (with UBIT, depreciation recapture, and a Section 754 step-up), and Lisa's small business on Schedule C. Their case is assigned via queue routing to a generalist expert who has never processed a K-1 with partnership-level adjustments. The expert enters the K-1 ordinary income on the correct line but misses the Section 199A qualified business income deduction, does not carry the depreciation recapture to the correct form, and omits the 754 adjustment entirely. The resulting return understates their QBI deduction by $4,200 and misstates their basis in the partnership. David, who is familiar with K-1s from his day job in private equity, reviews the draft and catches the errors. He spends the next three weeks in a back-and-forth with support trying to get the return corrected before filing. Total cost: $4,200 in missed deduction, 18 hours of David's time, and the couple switches to a local CPA firm the following year.

**What the proposed platform would have done differently:** The complexity scoring engine (B3) would have assigned this return a hard complexity flag for the K-1 with partnership adjustments. The specialty match routing (B5) would have routed the case to an expert with K-1 and partnership experience, or at minimum assigned a peer reviewer with that specialty. The recommendation engine (B1) would have surfaced the Section 199A deduction as a ranked suggestion tied to the Chens' goal of maximizing their refund.

## Vignette 4: The Lost Continuity (Theme 4 --- Hand-Off and Continuity Loss)

**Eleanor Briggs**, 62, is a retired teacher with a pension, Social Security, a Roth conversion ladder, and a rental duplex she has owned for 19 years. She used TurboTax Live Full Service for TY2023 and TY2024. Her TY2023 expert, whom she describes as excellent, understood the Roth conversion timing, the rental depreciation schedule, and the interaction between her pension and Social Security taxation. For TY2025, Eleanor logs in and is assigned a new expert with no access to the prior expert's notes, no visibility into the TY2024 return, and no awareness of the Roth conversion ladder strategy. The new expert asks Eleanor to re-explain her entire financial situation. The expert then makes a depreciation calculation error on the rental property --- the same property whose depreciation schedule was correctly handled the previous year. Eleanor catches it only because she kept her own spreadsheet. She asks to speak with her TY2024 expert and is told that is not possible. Total cost: $1,100 in depreciation error (caught before filing, but only because Eleanor is unusually diligent), 6 hours of re-explanation, and a deep sense that the platform does not value the relationship.

**What the proposed platform would have done differently:** The multi-year co-pilot (B2) would have maintained Eleanor's full financial context, including the Roth conversion ladder plan, the rental depreciation schedule, and the prior expert's notes. The continuity routing preference (I2) would have attempted to match Eleanor with the same expert or, failing that, an expert with full access to the prior year case file. The goal dashboard (B1) would have shown Eleanor's stated goals from TY2024 --- "optimize Roth conversion timing" and "maximize rental deductions" --- so the new expert would have started with context, not a blank slate.

## Vignette 5: The Four-Hour Wait (Theme 5 --- Long Wait Times and Queue Pain)

**James Okonkwo**, 29, is filing his first return with equity compensation after joining a startup. He has ISOs exercised in TY2025, a mix of qualifying and disqualifying dispositions, and potential AMT exposure. He signs up for TurboTax Live Assisted, expecting to ask a few targeted questions during preparation. On April 8, he initiates a live chat session. The estimated wait is 20 minutes. After 55 minutes, the chat disconnects. He tries the callback feature; the callback is scheduled for 3pm the next day. At 3pm, no call arrives. He calls the support number directly and waits 1 hour 40 minutes. The expert who answers is a product expert --- trained on TurboTax navigation, not tax law --- and cannot answer his ISO question. They offer to transfer him to a "tax specialist," which requires re-entering the queue. James waits another 50 minutes before reaching a credentialed expert who can address his AMT question. Total elapsed time: approximately 4.5 hours across three attempts. The expert interaction itself takes 12 minutes. Dollar impact: James nearly missed a $2,800 AMT credit carryforward because the product expert initially told him AMT "usually does not apply to people in your income range."

**What the proposed platform would have done differently:** The complexity scoring (B3) would have flagged James's ISO and AMT situation at intake, before he entered any queue. The specialty routing (B5) would have placed him directly in a queue for an expert with ISO and AMT experience, bypassing the generalist and product expert tiers entirely. The goal capture (B1) would have identified "understand my ISO tax impact" as his primary goal, ensuring the recommendation engine surfaced the AMT credit carryforward as a top-ranked item. The capacity dashboard (I2) would have given queue managers visibility into specialist utilization, reducing the likelihood of a 50-minute specialist wait during peak season.
